# Things to Consider

## Reap Culture Context

Reap is a tech scale-up. The review culture values radical honesty, open feedback, and directness with empathy. Reviews should feel like a real conversation, not a compliance exercise.

## The Five Company Values ("The Reap 5")

Full value definitions are in `inputs/company-values.md`. Below is what to look for when evaluating each value. Note: 4 are aspirational, 1 is core.

### 1. Own the Outcome [aspirational]
**Look for:** Bias to action. Closing the loop — not "almosts." Owning problems end-to-end including the finish line. Stepping outside job description to clear blockers. Quality as a signature. Seeking the best outcome for Reap, not just their team. Humility — nothing is beneath them.
**Red flags:** Passing the ball. Waiting for permission. Silo behavior. Turf-guarding. Excuses over results.

### 2. Start with the Customer [aspirational]
**Look for:** Relentless curiosity about customer problems. Digging into the *why* beneath requests. Validating with real users and data before building. Cutting work that doesn't move a customer metric. Delivering effortless experiences.
**Red flags:** Building for internal convenience. Rubber-stamping feature wish-lists. Skipping validation. Losing sight of who the customer actually is.

### 3. Speak Up, Team Up [aspirational]
**Look for:** Being both a committed teammate AND a thoughtful challenger. Answering Slack pings, sharing information liberally. Surfacing blind spots. Putting ideas on the table even when unpopular. Debating the idea while respecting the person. Showing up for others regardless of seniority.
**Red flags:** Silence when they have an opinion. Nodding along. Hiding behind titles. Partnership without pushback. Withholding information.

### 4. Think Deep, Move Fast [aspirational]
**Look for:** Startup-level urgency. Shipping and iterating. Pausing long enough to understand the real problem before sprinting. Documenting decision paths. Logging tech debt when shipping quick patches. Automating repetitive work. Treating missteps as fuel for improvement.
**Red flags:** Velocity without depth. Cutting corners that stay cut. Over-engineering without shipping. Not documenting decisions. Repeating the same mistakes.

### 5. Anything is Possible [core — this is who Reap IS]
**Look for:** Running toward complexity others avoid. Optimism-first reflex: "what would it take?" not "why won't it work?" Disciplined invention — bold ideas aligned to strategy. Setting bold goals, surfacing blockers early, pushing until live and scaling.
**Red flags:** Defaulting to "we can't." Dreaming without delivering. Ideas misaligned with strategy. Giving up at the first wall.

## Rating Scale: What Each Level Really Means

The competency rating scale (Not Meeting, Partially Meeting, Meeting, Exceeding, Legendary) carries real consequences. Understanding the actual weight of each level is critical for calibration.

**Not Meeting Expectations:** This is severe. It means the person is fundamentally not doing their job. Someone receiving this rating is almost certainly facing termination or a PIP. Reserve this for clear, sustained failure to perform core responsibilities.

**Partially Meeting Expectations:** Also a serious signal. This applies when someone has received consistent feedback to improve in the same area and there is no clear sign of improvement. The bar at Reap is high; this means the person is not operating at the level expected of them. Two or more "Partially Meeting" ratings across competencies should trigger a recommendation for a performance improvement plan.

**Meeting Expectations:** This is a good rating. It means the person is doing their job well and operating at the high bar Reap sets. Everyone at Reap is expected to be proactive, collaborative, and to push each other. Meeting that bar is genuinely solid performance.

**Exceeding Expectations:** This is a star performer. Someone others look up to. They consistently do more than what is asked, and they have outsized impact beyond their own scope, influencing other teams, other functions, or the broader organization.

**Legendary:** Extremely rare. Top 1% of the company. Legendary requires universal agreement; it is only valid if everyone who works with this person considers them legendary. This person is the role model that every employee should aspire to be. **Do not assign Legendary yourself.** You can suggest it in the coaching notes as something for the manager to consider, but never set it as a rating in the review draft.

## The Three Competency Areas

### Job Mastery
- Domain expertise and craft quality
- Trade-off reasoning (simplicity vs. complexity, speed vs. correctness)
- Communication of decisions through appropriate formats
- Challenging assumptions and finding better approaches
- Learning from feedback and improving over time

### Execution and Outcomes
- Hitting commitments and controlling scope
- Proactive planning and adaptation
- Owning and resolving blockers
- Transparency on progress and accountability for results
- Measurable customer or business impact tied to roadmap/OKRs

### People, Collaboration and Influence
- Active listening and clear communication
- Handling feedback, disagreement, and conflict constructively
- Building trusted, inclusive working relationships
- Cross-team collaboration and stakeholder alignment
- Mentoring and strengthening others' capabilities
- Influencing without authority

## Anti-Patterns to Watch For in Evidence Gathering

- **Recency bias** — don't over-weight the last month. Look at the full review period.
- **Visibility bias** — loud contributions aren't always the most valuable. Look for quiet, high-impact work.
- **Halo/horn effect** — one great (or bad) project shouldn't color everything. Evaluate each dimension independently.
- **Self-review anchoring** — the self-review is a reference, not a starting point. Don't let it frame the narrative.
